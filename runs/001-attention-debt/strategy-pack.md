\# Strategy Pack — Attention Debt and AI Communication



\## Recommended angle



AI has made workplace communication cheaper to create, but organisational attention is still expensive.



The real productivity opportunity is not generating more messages, updates, summaries, or slide packs. It is reducing the communication burden created by easy content generation.



\## One-line thesis



The next wave of AI productivity will come from reducing attention debt, not increasing content volume.



\## Target audience



Primary audience:



\- Project Managers

\- Business Analysts

\- Change Managers

\- AI governance leads

\- Delivery leaders



Secondary audience:



\- Internal communications teams

\- Product owners

\- Transformation leads

\- Executives dealing with information overload



\## Why this matters



AI tools are making it easier to produce workplace content at scale.



That includes emails, meeting notes, project updates, summaries, reports, briefing packs, and internal posts.



The risk is that organisations mistake more communication for better communication.



In reality, every message creates a receiving cost. Someone has to read it, interpret it, decide whether it matters, and work out whether action is needed.



If AI increases content volume without improving relevance, clarity, and decision value, it creates attention debt.



\## Core argument



The content bottleneck is being solved.



The attention bottleneck is getting worse.



Good AI adoption should therefore include subtraction, not just generation.



Teams need to ask:



\- Does this message need to exist?

\- Who actually needs it?

\- What decision or action should it support?

\- Can the message be shorter, clearer, or replaced by a dashboard, decision log, or exception report?

\- What noise are we creating by making communication easier to produce?



\## Supporting points



\### 1. Content production is no longer the hard part



Generative AI makes it easy to produce first drafts, summaries, status updates, meeting notes, and polished internal content.



That shifts the scarce resource from writing effort to reader attention.



\### 2. Every message has a receiving cost



A message that takes five minutes to create may create thirty minutes of combined reading, interpretation, forwarding, clarification, and decision cost.



That cost is usually invisible.



\### 3. AI can amplify noise if governance focuses only on usage



Many AI adoption programs focus on prompts, productivity, and acceptable use.



They also need to consider communication load, review burden, and decision quality.



\### 4. Subtraction is a serious productivity move



The best use of AI may not be “write this update”.



It may be:



\- compress this update

\- remove duplication

\- identify who actually needs this

\- turn this into a decision note

\- convert this into an exception report

\- flag what can be deleted



\## Alternative angles considered



\### Angle 1: AI is creating workplace spam



Rejected.



Too negative and too broad. It risks sounding anti-AI and does not give enough practical direction.



\### Angle 2: Internal communications needs to change because of AI



Partially useful, but too narrow.



The issue is bigger than internal communications. It affects project delivery, change management, governance, reporting, and executive decision-making.



\### Angle 3: Attention debt is the next AI governance issue



Strong angle, but possibly too abstract for the first post.



Could be used as a follow-up article.



\### Angle 4: AI productivity needs subtraction as well as creation



Strong supporting frame.



This should be included in the final article.



\## Risks / sensitivities



\- Do not sound anti-AI.

\- Do not blame communication teams.

\- Do not imply fewer updates are always better.

\- Do not overclaim that AI is causing the whole problem.

\- Avoid making the article sound like a complaint about email.

\- Keep the tone practical, not preachy.



\## What to avoid



Avoid phrases like:



\- “AI is flooding the workplace with junk”

\- “communication teams are the problem”

\- “nobody reads anything anymore”

\- “AI-generated content is worthless”

\- “this changes everything”



Avoid generic thought-leadership language like:



\- paradigm shift

\- unlock productivity

\- future of work

\- game changer

\- transformative potential

\## Builder instructions



The Builder should produce practical, plain-English outputs.



Tone:



\- senior

\- grounded

\- useful

\- lightly opinionated

\- not anti-AI

\- not hype-driven



The article should open with the shift:



> AI has made workplace communication easier to create. It has not made workplace attention easier to manage.



The post should end with a practical question, such as:



> Before we ask AI to write the message, maybe we should ask whether the message should exist.
